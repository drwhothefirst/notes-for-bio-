01

Endocrine System

Ductless glands · hormones via blood · slow but long-lasting control
Major endocrine glands — schematic locations

1.1 Hormones & Glands — basics

  • Hormone = chemical messenger secreted directly into blood that binds a receptor in a target tissue to change its function.
  • Target cells carry the matching receptor; one hormone can have many target cells, and a cell can hold receptors for more than one hormone.
  • Endocrine glands are ductless → secrete into the bloodstream. Exocrine glands have ducts (e.g. salivary, sweat glands).
  • Mixed glands (both functions): the pancreas and gonads.
  • Steroid hormones — made from cholesterol (a lipid); lipid-soluble, cross the membrane and bind receptors in the cytoplasm. Example: estrogen, testosterone, progesterone.
  • Non-steroid hormones — amino acids / peptides / proteins; not lipid-soluble, so bind cell-surface receptors. Examples: epinephrine, norepinephrine, growth hormone.

1.2 Hypothalamus & Pituitary

  • The hypothalamus links the nervous & endocrine systems; its releasing/inhibiting hormones control the anterior pituitary.
  • Oxytocin and ADH are made in the hypothalamus but stored & released by the posterior pituitary.

1.3 Thyroid · Parathyroid · Thymus · Pineal

  • Pineal → melatonin, regulating the circadian (sleep–wake) rhythm.
  • Thyroid sits below the larynx, butterfly-shaped. Follicular cells make T3 & T4 (growth, development, metabolism). Parafollicular (C) cells make calcitonin, which lowers blood calcium.
  • Parathyroid — 4 glands on the posterior thyroid. PTH raises blood calcium: activates osteoclasts (remove Ca from bone) and boosts Ca absorption in kidney & intestine.
  • Thymus — behind the sternum between the lungs; key in childhood. Releases thymosin → matures T lymphocytes.

1.4 Adrenal Gland

  • Also called suprarenal glands — sit on top of each kidney.
  • Cortex (outer) = steroid hormones; medulla (inner) = catecholamines.

  • Adrenal medulla → epinephrine + norepinephrine during the fight-or-flight response: ↑ heart rate, ↑ blood pressure, ↑ blood glucose, ↑ respiratory rate.

1.5 Pancreas & Gonads

  • Endocrine pancreas = islets of Langerhans; exocrine = acini (digestive enzymes).
  • Alpha → glucagon (raises glucose). Beta → insulin (lowers glucose). Delta → somatostatin (regulates digestion).
  • Gonads are mixed: sex hormones (endocrine) + gametes/sperm & ova (exocrine).
  • Ovaries → estrogen (secondary sex characteristics) & progesterone (prepares uterus for pregnancy).
  • Testes — interstitial cells → testosterone (secondary sex characteristics + sperm production).

1.6 Feedback Mechanisms & Diseases

  • Negative feedback — output reduces the original stimulus. Thyroid loop: ↓T3/T4 → hypothalamus releases TRH → pituitary releases TSH → thyroid releases T3/T4.
  • Positive feedback — output enhances the original effect. Example: suckling → prolactin → milk secretion (amplifies).
Cushing Syndrome ↑ Cortisol

Long-term high cortisol. Signs: weight gain at the waist/face/upper back, thin easily-bruised skin, severe fatigue, muscle weakness, depression & anxiety.

Graves' Disease Autoimmune · Hyperthyroid

Immune system drives the thyroid to overproduce hormone. Signs: bulging eyes, anxiety, heat intolerance, difficulty sleeping.

Nervous System

Fast electrical signalling · neurons + glia · CNS & PNS
Neuron — impulse travels dendrite → cell body → axon → terminals

2.1 Neurons & Neuroglia

  • Nervous tissue = neurons (functional units, carry signals) + neuroglia (support & nourish).
  • Neuron parts: dendrites (carry impulses toward soma), cell body/soma (nucleus + organelles), axon (carries impulses away). Myelin sheath = insulating lipid layer.

2.2 CNS · Brain & Spinal Cord

  • CNS = brain + spinal cord. PNS = cranial nerves + spinal nerves.
  • Meninges (outer → inner): Dura → Arachnoid → Pia mater. CSF fills the spaces, cushions & barriers against microbes.
  • Four brain parts: cerebrum, cerebellum, brain stem, diencephalon (not the hippocampus).
  • Cerebrum — largest; 2 hemispheres; 4 lobes: frontal, parietal, temporal, occipital.
  • Cerebellum — back of brain; balance, posture & learning motor skills.
  • Brain stem — connects brain to cord; = midbrain + pons + medulla oblongata; controls heart rate, breathing, blood pressure.
  • Diencephalon — relay for sensory & motor; holds thalamus + hypothalamus (controls pituitary, circadian rhythm, temperature, BP, heart rate).
  • Spinal cord — ends ~T12–L1; 31 segments, each giving a pair of spinal nerves; transmits signals between brain & body.

2.3 PNS & Autonomic Nervous System

  • 12 pairs cranial nerves (sensory, motor, or mixed); 31 pairs spinal nerves.
  • Spinal nerve pairs: cervical 8 · thoracic 12 · lumbar 5 · sacral 5 · coccygeal 1.
  • PNS splits into somatic (voluntary skeletal muscle) and autonomic (cardiac/smooth muscle, organs, glands).

2.4 Reflex Arc & Synapse

  • Reflex = rapid, involuntary reaction. Reflex arc path: receptor → sensory neuron → integration center (brain/cord) → motor neuron → effector.
  • Nerve impulse = electrical signal, travels dendrite → cell body → axon.
  • Synapse — junction between neurons (or neuron–muscle/gland). The gap = synaptic cleft; presynaptic end-bulb stores neurotransmitters; receptors sit on the postsynaptic membrane.
  • Chemical synapse: Ca²⁺ enters terminal → vesicles fuse → neurotransmitter released → binds receptor proteins. Electrical synapse: current passes directly through channels.
  • Clearing the cleft: enzyme breakdown (acetylcholinesterase for ACh) or reabsorption by the presynaptic membrane.
  • Named neurotransmitters: acetylcholine & adrenaline.

2.5 Nervous System Disorders

Multiple Sclerosis (MS) Autoimmune · Demyelinating

Immune attack on a protein in the myelin sheath → disrupts signal transmission. Signs: double vision/vision loss, muscle weakness, loss of balance.

Alzheimer's Disease Neurodegenerative

Causes ~60–70% of dementia. Beta-amyloid deposits around neurons. Earliest sign: difficulty remembering recent events; later: language problems, disorientation, mood swings, self-neglect.

03

Respiratory System

Moves air for gas exchange · O₂ in, CO₂ out
Airway: nose → pharynx → larynx → trachea → bronchi → lungs (alveoli)

3.1 Upper tract — nose to larynx

  • Main job: move air in/out for gas exchange — take in oxygen, remove CO₂.
  • Organs: nose, pharynx, larynx, trachea, bronchi, lungs (alveoli). The esophagus is not respiratory.
  • Nose — only externally visible part; cavity split by the nasal septum; lined with respiratory mucosa that moistens air & traps particles. Olfactory receptors sit on the superior mucosa. Conchae ↑ surface area. The palate separates nasal from oral cavity.
  • 4 pairs of paranasal sinuses — frontal, sphenoid, ethmoid, maxillary (not temporal). They lighten the skull & add voice resonance.
  • Pharynx — common path for air & food. Regions: nasopharynx (behind nose), oropharynx, laryngopharynx (→ larynx).
  • Larynx — largest cartilage = thyroid cartilage (Adam's apple). Space between vocal cords = glottis. Epiglottis covers the larynx during swallowing → stops food entering the trachea.

3.2 Lower tract & lungs

  • Trachea — reinforced by C-shaped hyaline cartilage (stops collapse); lined with pseudostratified ciliated columnar epithelium.
  • Bronchioles <1 mm; terminal bronchioles <0.5 mm.
  • Respiratory zone = respiratory bronchioles + alveolar ducts + alveoli (functional units; gas exchange by diffusion). Conducting zone = everything except alveoli.
  • Right lung = 3 lobes, left lung = 2 lobes. Base rests on the diaphragm; apex near the clavicle.
  • Pleura: parietal lines the thoracic wall, visceral covers the lung. Inter-pleural fluid lets them glide.
  • Tract split: upper = nose, pharynx, larynx; lower = trachea, bronchi, bronchioles, alveoli, lungs.

3.3 Mechanics & types of respiration

  • Diaphragm — dome-shaped muscle separating thoracic & abdominal cavities. Intercostals ↑ the antero-posterior chest diameter.
  • Inspiration = active: diaphragm contracts, flattens, moves down. Expiration = passive: muscles relax.
  • External respiration — alveoli ↔ pulmonary capillaries. Internal respiration — blood ↔ tissues. Cellular respiration — in mitochondria; uses O₂ to make ATP, produces CO₂.
Pneumonia Infection

Inflames the alveoli, which fill with fluid/pus. Signs: cough, high fever with chills & sweating, shortness of breath, chest pain, fatigue.

Bronchitis Inflammation

Inflammation of the bronchial tubes; most commonly viral.

04

Urinary System

Renal system · filters blood · ~180 L/day · makes urine
Nephron — filtrate path: glomerulus → Bowman's → PCT → loop → DCT → collecting duct

4.1 Functions & hormones

  • Also called the renal system. Organs: kidneys, ureters, bladder, urethra.
  • ~180 L filtered daily; only a fraction becomes urine. Nitrogenous wastes: urea, creatinine, uric acid.
  • Acid–base balance: excrete H⁺, reabsorb bicarbonate; keep blood pH 7.35–7.45.
  • Erythropoietin — released when kidneys sense low oxygen → stimulates RBC production in bone marrow.

4.2 Kidney & nephron anatomy

  • Kidneys are bean-shaped, protected by perirenal fat. Outer = cortex, inner = medulla. Pyramids drain into the renal pelvis → ureter.
  • Hilum — where the renal artery enters and the renal vein + ureter exit. Blood enters via the renal artery.
  • Each kidney has >1 million nephrons (functional units). Cortical nephrons ≈ 85%; juxtamedullary ≈ 15% (cortex–medulla margin).
  • Filtration at the glomerulus → Bowman's capsule → PCT. Proteins & blood cells are too large to filter (stay in blood); water, salts, glucose, amino acids, urea pass through. The fluid is the filtrate.
  • Tubular secretion — final adjustments & pH control by secreting H⁺.

4.3 Ureters, bladder & urination

  • Ureters — ~25 cm; smooth muscle creates peristalsis to push urine to the bladder.
  • Bladder — lined with transitional epithelium (stretches); has 3 openings (2 ureters + 1 urethra).
  • Sphincters: internal = smooth muscle (involuntary); external = skeletal muscle (voluntary).
  • Micturition (urination) reflex — bladder fills → cord signals → detrusor muscle contracts, internal sphincter relaxes.
Polycystic Kidney Disease (PKD) Genetic

Gene changes → many cysts grow in the kidneys. Signs: high BP, back/side pain, hematuria, kidney stones, eventual failure.

Acute Kidney Failure Sudden

Rapid loss of function over hours–days; can be life-threatening. Oliguria/anuria = decreased/absent urine output.

Chronic Kidney Failure Gradual

Most common cause = diabetes mellitus, then high blood pressure.

05

Cardiovascular System

Heart + blood vessels + blood · transports throughout the body
Heart chambers, valves & great vessels — right side to lungs, left side to body

5.1 Heart wall & pericardium

  • Components: heart, blood vessels, blood.
  • Pericardium (sac): outer fibrous pericardium (strong connective tissue); serous layers — visceral = epicardium (on the heart). Pericardial space holds serous fluid (prevents friction).
  • Heart wall layers: epicardium (outer, connective + fat) · myocardium (middle, cardiac muscle — does the contracting) · endocardium (inner, lines chambers & valves).

5.2 Chambers, valves & blood flow

  • Atria = thin-walled upper chambers (receive from veins), split by the interatrial septum. Ventricles = thick-walled lower chambers (pump out), split by the interventricular septum.
The four valves
ValveLocation
Tricuspidright atrium → right ventricle
Bicuspid (mitral)left atrium → left ventricle
Pulmonary semilunarright ventricle → pulmonary artery

  • AV valves (tri + bi) prevent backflow into the atria when ventricles contract.
  • Flow: vena cava → RA → tricuspid → RV → pulmonary valve → pulmonary arteries → lungs → pulmonary veins → LA → mitral → LV → aortic valve → aorta → body.
  • Aorta = largest artery. Pulmonary arteries = only arteries carrying deoxygenated blood; pulmonary veins = only veins carrying oxygenated blood. Coronary arteries feed the heart muscle. Vena cava = largest veins.
  • Pulmonary circuit = heart ↔ lungs. Systemic circuit = heart ↔ rest of body.

5.3 Cardiac cycle & conduction

  • Cardiac cycle = one full heartbeat; ~70/min at rest. Systole = contraction; diastole = relaxation.
  • Heart sounds: S1 "lub" = AV valves close; S2 "dub" = semilunar valves close.
  • Conduction path: SA node (pacemaker) → AV node → Bundle of His (AV bundle) → Purkinje fibers → ventricles contract.

5.4 Blood vessels & blood

  • Arteries carry blood away from the heart (thick walls). Veins have valves → flow toward the heart. Capillaries = smallest, link arteries↔veins, one-cell-thick (tunica intima only) for exchange.
  • Vessel wall layers: tunica intima (inner endothelium) · tunica media (smooth muscle + elastic) · tunica adventitia (outer connective tissue).
  • Systolic BP = max pressure during ventricular contraction; diastolic = min pressure when ventricles relax.
  • Plasma ≈ 92% water, 7% proteins, 1% other.
  • RBCs (erythrocytes) — carry O₂ via hemoglobin; made in red bone marrow; live ~120 days; removed by spleen & liver.
  • WBCs (leukocytes) — immunity. Granular: neutrophils, eosinophils, basophils. Agranular: lymphocytes, monocytes.
  • Platelets (thrombocytes) — fragments that secrete clotting factors. Three blood functions: transportation, regulation, protection.
Cardiomyopathy Heart muscle

Disease of the heart muscle; early stages may be symptom-free. Acquired causes: long-term high BP, heart-attack damage, metabolic disorders.

Arteriosclerosis / Atherosclerosis Vessels

Arteriosclerosis = hardening/stiffening of artery walls. Atherosclerosis = buildup of fats, cholesterol & other substances (plaque). Signs: chest pain, shortness of breath, dizziness, limb weakness.

Immune & Lymphatic System

Three lines of defense · B & T cells · antibodies · lymph
Three lines of defense + the Y-shaped antibody

6.1 Innate immunity — lines of defense

  • 1st line (barriers) — skin, mucous membranes, tears, saliva, sebum, gastric juice. Non-specific; stops entry. Saliva & tears carry antibacterial enzymes (lysozyme).
  • 2nd line (innate, internal) — phagocytes, natural killer cells, macrophages, antimicrobial proteins, interferons, complement proteins, fever, inflammation. Acts after entry; still non-specific.
  • 3rd line (adaptive) — lymphocytes, antibodies, memory cells. Specific. So: lines 1 & 2 = innate/non-specific; line 3 = adaptive/specific.

6.2 B & T cells (adaptive)

  • Adaptive immunity depends on lymphocytes → B cells & T cells.
  • Cell-mediated immunity — T cells destroy cells presenting an antigen. Antibody-mediated (humoral) immunity — B cells make antibodies against free antigens.
  • B cell clonal expansion — triggered when the BCR shape fits the antigen. Most clones → plasma cells (secrete antibodies); some → memory B cells (long-term immunity, fast re-response → basis of vaccination).
  • Cytotoxic T cells — directly kill abnormal/foreign/virus-infected cells. Helper T cells — secrete cytokines that activate other immune cells.

  • Opsonization — antibodies coat a pathogen so phagocytes engulf it more easily.
  • Active immunity — your own plasma cells make antibodies (long-lasting). Passive immunity — antibodies from outside (e.g. newborn via breast milk).
Type I Diabetes Mellitus Autoimmune

Immune system attacks the pancreas → impaired insulin production.

HIV / AIDS Viral

HIV infects & inactivates helper T cells, progressively weakening immunity.

6.4 Lymphatic system

  • Lymph — clear fluid from interstitial fluid; rich in lymphocytes. No central pump → moved by skeletal muscle contraction + one-way valves, flowing only toward the heart.
  • Thoracic duct — drains below the thorax + left arm & left head/neck → left subclavian vein. Right lymphatic duct — right arm + right head/neck → right subclavian vein.
  • Primary lymphatic organs — red bone marrow & thymus (lymphocytes form/mature). T cells mature in the thymus (helped by thymosin).
  • Spleen — largest lymphatic organ; red pulp filters blood & stores RBCs, white pulp = immune. Peyer's patches — lymphatic tissue in the ileum.
Lymphedema Fluid buildup

Abnormal lymph buildup (usually arms/legs) from underdeveloped vessels (primary) or damage from surgery/radiation/infection (secondary).

07

Digestive System

GI tract mouth → anus · accessory organs · enzymes
GI tract + accessory organs (liver, pancreas)

7.1 GI tract

  • Continuous tube from the oral cavity (mouth) to the anus.
  • Wall layers (inner → outer): mucosa (epithelial) · submucosa · muscularis · serosa.
  • Peristalsis — involuntary wave-like contractions that propel food.
  • Salivary amylase begins starch digestion in the mouth. In the stomach, HCl converts pepsinogen → active pepsin.
  • Pyloric sphincter — controls chyme exit from stomach → duodenum.
  • Small intestine: duodenum (first, from pylorus) → jejunum → ileum. Villi = nutrient absorption units.
  • Large intestine — absorbs water, forms feces. Rectum = last part before the anus.

7.2 Accessory organs & enzymes

  • Liver makes bile; the gallbladder only stores & concentrates it. Liver also stores glycogen, stores vitamins/minerals, and detoxifies. Bilirubin (bile pigment) comes from breaking down hemoglobin.
  • Bile salts emulsify fats into smaller droplets (don't break proteins).
  • Pancreas = mixed gland: exocrine enzymes + endocrine islets (insulin). Lipase breaks down fats.
  • Three major salivary gland pairs: parotid, submandibular, sublingual.

7.3 GI Diseases

Celiac Disease Autoimmune

Immune reaction to gluten (wheat, barley, rye) damages the villi of the small intestine → poor absorption.

Crohn's Disease & Ulcerative Colitis IBD

Crohn's — ulcers anywhere, typically small intestine + upper large intestine. Ulcerative colitis — starts in the rectum, spreads through the colon. Shared signs: chronic diarrhea, abdominal pain, blood in stool, fatigue.
